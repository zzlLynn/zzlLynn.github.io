Pain Points

based on NYTM's current site

Current Site
After careful user research and interviews, combined with the features of NYTM's original website, the following pain points were discovered.
Most visitors are confused by the types of museum tickets, which they cannot distinguish on their first visit.
The membership portal is indiscoverable with unclear corresponding benefits.
On the lengthy ticketing page, the Call-to-action button is at the bottom, which makes users lose patience and get distracted.
Too much information is listed and the hierarchy is confusing, without a standardized and consistent design system, leaving users uncertain about the next action.
The responsive pages on mobile are below expectations.

Constructing High-Fidelity Prototypes

Feature 1 - Interactive Visual Cues

hints and guidance to make the information clearer

The date and time section is not displayed (Use translucent state to imply unclickable) until the number and tvpe of tickets are confirmed, to give the user a more directed step.

Similarly, the "Add to Order" button remains translucent and unclickable until the contents of the "Type & Number of Tickets" & "Date & Time" have been entered. This prevents the user from forgetting to select the key content because the page is too long.

Feature 2 - Collapsible Panel

increase interactivity and streamline page content

Collapsible panels are an important element in the consistency of web design.

Combining the need for users to avoid excessively long pages with best practices for displaying FAQs on competitors' sites, we decided to use drop-down menus to hide and display content.

Feature 3 - Rational Organization

keep users informed overall

Hyperlinks are added to link ticket purchases to membership.

In addition, a variety of ticketing options can be seen before the ticket purchase portal, giving users a clearer overview. It is easier to compare and find the most suitable ticket type. These clickable sections are created with signifiers, so the users can make an informed decision that caters to their visit.

When displaying different membership tiers, we decided to put similar types of memberships side by side so users can more easily compare prices and benefits.

For mobile, we chose to combine card stacking to streamline the page length while adding interactivity.

my takeaways

The NYTM website's navigation and organization system has been redesigned after three months of research, focusing specifically on the ticketing and membership page. This model has not been widely tested with users. In order to achieve an inclusive design, further tests and research should be conducted in the future.

This project provided me with a more systematic learning of a series of methodologies for user research and familiarized me with the logic and procedures from user demand to deliverable design.
